About
About This Album & About Me

I release music as CyclicElegy. This project, The Monster that lives on 2120 Samuel Drive, isn’t just an album – it’s evidence. It’s the record of what happened to me inside a relationship with a narcissist, and what it did to a mind already carrying ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia.

I didn’t walk into that house empty. I walked in with a life, a sense of self, some kind of shaky self‑respect. I walked out of it feeling like a ghost in my own story. By the end, my brain didn’t feel like a place I lived in – it felt like a prison I wanted to escape at any cost.

These songs come from that place: love‑bombed, gaslit, blamed, torn apart, then left alone with suicidal thoughts that felt like the only way to shut it all off. This isn’t pretty, it’s not polished, and it’s not designed to make her look bad. It’s designed to make the truth impossible to ignore.

I’m not a therapist, I’m the person who almost didn’t make it out. This album is me pinning the story to the wall so it can’t be rewritten again. If you hear your own life in any of this, I want you to know: you’re not crazy, you’re not overreacting, and you’re allowed to want more than surviving somebody else’s damage.

This project deals with heavy topics, including emotional abuse and suicidal thoughts. If you’re standing where I once stood and you’re tired of breathing but still here, please reach out to someone you trust, a mental health professional, or a crisis service in your area. Your story is not over just because it feels unbearable right now.

My Story
Living With My Mind

This album is tied directly to my mental health story. I’m currently diagnosed and medicated for ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia. A lot of this didn’t fully show itself until I went through a relationship with a narcissist that pushed my mind past what it could quietly carry.

For a long time I felt like my brain was a war zone: racing thoughts, emotional highs and lows, and eventually a break from reality that I didn’t see coming. The relationship didn’t “create” everything on its own, but it was like a trigger that flipped switches in my mind I didn’t know were there.

ADHD makes focusing and staying organized feel like trying to hold onto smoke. Bipolar moods can swing from feeling invincible to feeling like I’m underwater. Schizophrenia added another layer: thoughts, perceptions, and fears that sometimes don’t match what’s really in front of me.

Writing this album became a way to pull all of that chaos out of my head and put it somewhere else – into songs, lyrics, and sound. It doesn’t cure anything, but it gives me a way to make sense of what I live with every day.

This is my personal experience, not medical advice. If anything in my story feels familiar to you, please reach out to a trusted person or a mental health professional. You’re not weak for needing help.

Definitions
The Terms I Live With

These are simple explanations of the mental health conditions I live with and take medication for. They’re not perfect textbook definitions, but they’re how I understand and experience them.

ADHD (Attention‑Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)
For me, ADHD feels like having a hundred radio stations on in my head at once. It shows up as:
  • Racing thoughts and trouble focusing on one thing at a time.
  • Starting projects with intense energy, then burning out fast.
  • Forgetfulness, disorganization, and time slipping away.
It doesn’t mean I’m lazy or don’t care — my brain just runs on a different frequency.
Bipolar Disorder
Bipolar, for me, is living between emotional extremes:
  • Highs (mania or hypomania) where I feel wired, driven, or invincible.
  • Lows (depression) where everything feels heavy, slow, and pointless.
These aren’t just “mood swings” — they can affect sleep, judgment, decisions, and how I see myself and the world.
Schizophrenia
Schizophrenia, the hardest one to talk about, is about my connection to reality sometimes warping. For me, it can involve:
  • Thoughts that feel jumbled or pulled in strange directions.
  • Perceptions or fears that don’t always match what’s really happening.
  • Moments of paranoia or feeling watched/judged when I’m not.
It doesn’t make me a monster; it means my brain sometimes misfires in ways that can be frightening and exhausting.

All of these conditions exist alongside what I went through in a narcissistic relationship. The stress and abuse didn’t “create” them, but they did trigger and intensify symptoms in ways that changed my life and my mind.

This section is based on my personal experience. For clinical definitions or if you’re worried about yourself, please look up trusted mental health resources or talk to a professional.

Narcissism 101
What Is Narcissism?

When I talk about narcissism here, I’m not talking about someone who just takes too many selfies or likes attention. I’m talking about the kind of person who will burn your mind down to protect the image they have of themselves.

The narcissism I lived through was an addiction to being admired and seen as “good” at any cost. The mask always had to stay perfect. If I pointed out cracks in it, I became the problem, the villain, the “crazy” one. It wasn’t just selfishness – it was a full‑time war against my reality to protect hers.

How It Showed Up With Her
  • In public: charming, caring, innocent. Online: carefully curated. In private: cold, unforgiving, and vicious when I didn’t play my role.
  • My feelings were props. If I cried, it was “dramatic.” If I was hurt, I was “too sensitive.” If I tried to leave, I was “ungrateful.”
  • Apologies, when they happened, were just damage control to keep people from seeing who she really was. Nothing actually changed.
  • Somehow every argument twisted into being my fault, even when I was reacting to something she’d done.
Narcissistic Supply: Being Used as Fuel

People like her talk about “love,” but what they really need is supply. Narcissistic supply is the attention, admiration, reaction, and emotional energy they feed on. You’re not a partner to them – you’re a battery. And when you stop powering their ego the way they want, they either punish you or replace you.

You get chosen because you have something they want: empathy, talent, vulnerability, status, looks, stability – something they can drain or wear like a costume. You get molded into the role they need you to play: the savior, the caretaker, the project, the punching bag. You get discarded when you stop feeding the fantasy or start seeing through it.

And then, the worst part: you get recycled. When their new supply starts to run out, or when they need a hit of the power they had over you, they circle back – a message, a memory, an apology, a fake confession. Not because they miss you, but because they miss what you gave them.

I’m not here to hand out diagnoses, but I am here to say this: being tangled up with someone like this can eat away at your mental health until you don’t trust your own thoughts. That’s the kind of narcissism this album is about.

This is me speaking as someone who lived it, not as a therapist. If these patterns feel uncomfortably familiar, please start reading about narcissistic abuse and gaslighting from trusted sources. Knowledge doesn’t fix everything, but it can be the first crack in the cage.

The Dark Triad
Narcissism, Manipulation & Coldness

The “dark triad” sounds like some academic buzzword, but when you’re on the receiving end of it, it’s not theory – it’s your life slowly getting ripped apart. It’s what happens when a person’s ego, need for control, and lack of conscience all team up.

Narcissism
Not just “I like myself.” This is “I must be seen as good, innocent, special, no matter who I have to hurt or lie to.” With her, it meant the outside world got the kind, soft version, and I got the one who would twist my reality to protect her reputation.
Machiavellianism
This is the cold, calculated part. Conversations were chess games I didn’t know I was playing. Information I shared in trust later came back as a weapon. Situations were staged so that if I reacted, I’d look unstable and she’d look like the victim.
Psychopathic traits
I’m not saying she’s a movie monster. I’m saying there was a kind of emotional emptiness there – a way she could watch me fall apart, then complain about how my pain was “too much” for her. The damage she did never seemed to land on her in any real way.

Put together, this isn’t just “a toxic relationship.” It’s a slow, methodical breakdown of who you think you are. You start doubting your memories, your reactions, your sanity, until you’re apologizing for the ways you’ve been hurt.

Reading about the dark triad didn’t magically fix me, but it gave me language for what I survived. It let me say: “No, this wasn’t just a bad breakup. This was calculated, this was cruel, and it did real damage to my mind.”

I’m sharing this as someone on the other side of it, not as a professional. If you recognize this pattern in your own life, believe yourself. It’s darker than most people want to admit, and you are not overreacting for wanting to get out.

The Dark Side
Inside the Abuse

The dark side of narcissism isn’t dramatic in a cool, movie way. It’s slow, ugly, and designed so that nobody believes you when you finally speak up. From the outside, it looks like a “normal” relationship. On the inside, it feels like you’re being erased one argument at a time.

How It Ate Me Alive
  • Gaslighting: being told I remembered things wrong, that conversations “never happened,” or that I was “crazy” for being hurt. Eventually I started to believe her more than my own memory.
  • Love‑bombing then devaluation: one day I was everything, the next I was nothing. The constant switch between “I adore you” and “you’re the problem” kept me addicted and off balance.
  • Control disguised as caring: “I’m just worried about you” turned into pressure over who I talked to, where I went, what I believed, how I reacted. My life stopped feeling like mine.
  • Blame‑shifting: every time I finally broke down, the focus became my reaction, not what she’d done. I ended up apologizing for being hurt.

Over time, this didn’t just mess with my feelings — it screwed with my mind. My ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia didn’t exist in a vacuum. The constant stress, fear, and confusion poured gasoline on all of it. My brain stopped feeling like a place I could safely live.

This album isn’t about “a rough breakup.” It’s about standing in the wreckage of what someone else did to you, and trying to recognize yourself in the mirror again.

With her, the split between public and private was huge. In front of other people, she worked hard to look kind, put‑together, and innocent — the last person who would ever be called a narcissist. But behind closed doors, the traits were there: the constant need to protect her image, the lack of real empathy for what I was feeling, and the way my pain was either ignored, minimized, or used against me. That gap between who she pretended to be and who she actually was almost destroyed me.

All of this played out in that house on that street – 2120 Samuel Drive. On the surface, it’s just an address. To me it’s a crime scene with no police report. Four walls that watched me give up my self‑respect piece by piece, trading it for the hope that she’d finally love me the way she pretended to in the beginning.

That’s the part people don’t want to face about narcissistic abuse: how dark it actually gets. It’s not just “toxic vibes.” It is the slow murder of your self‑worth. It’s you throwing yourself under the bus over and over just to keep the peace, until you don’t remember what it felt like to respect yourself.

The Other Side
What I Lived Through

Being on the other side of a narcissist doesn’t feel like “having relationship problems.” It feels like slowly dying in a room no one else can see. You start out defending them to everyone, and you end up not being able to defend yourself to yourself.

I went through emotional abuse, manipulation, and moments that crossed lines in ways I still feel in my body. I shrank myself down to keep the peace. I walked on eggshells in my own life, terrified of setting her off or being painted as the monster.

What It Really Felt Like
  • Waking up every day wondering which version of her I’d get, and what version of me I’d have to kill off to survive it.
  • Apologizing for things I didn’t do, just to stop the endless twisting, arguing, and emotional blackmail.
  • Feeling guilty for even thinking about leaving, because she’d done such a good job convincing me that everything was my fault.
  • Hiding the truth from people because I was ashamed, confused, and scared nobody would believe me or understand how bad it really was.

My mind carried the cost. My ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia got tangled up with her abuse until I couldn’t tell where my illness ended and her behavior began. There were days where escaping my own head felt harder than escaping her.

This isn’t a song cycle about heartbreak. It’s a record of what it’s like to be broken down slowly, told you’re the problem, and then left alone with the pieces.

If any of this sounds like your life, you’re not being dramatic. You’re not “too sensitive.” You’re reacting like a human being to something that is actually cruel. Wanting out doesn’t make you the villain — it makes you someone who still has a survival instinct left.

The worst part is how often people like this are never held accountable. They move on, polishing their image for the next audience, while you’re left sifting through the wreckage they left in your head. No charges, no consequences, just a trail of blood, sweat, and tears paved behind them from person to person, relationship to relationship.

The Monster that lives on 2120 Samuel Drive is me refusing to let that trail stay invisible. That street, that house, that version of me — they’re all part of the story. If she walks away looking clean, this album is the stain that tells the truth.

The Veil
When the Mask Finally Slips

Narcissists survive on a veil – a mask they wear so the world sees them as kind, wounded, special, misunderstood. That mask can stay on for years. In my case, I didn’t see it for what it was until almost two years in. By then, I was already tangled up in her story and half‑erased from my own.

At first, the mask was perfect: the right words, the right tears, the right “vulnerable” moments. She studied real human emotion like a script and then acted it out. Even when she cried – which was rare – it felt off, like someone doing an impression of sadness instead of actually feeling it.

It only started to crack when I began researching narcissism. I read about love‑bombing, gaslighting, devaluation, discard, recycling, supply – and it was like reading our relationship written by a stranger. She followed the narcissistic playbook almost verbatim. It got to the point where I could literally predict what she was going to do next.

The more I saw through the mask and called it out, the worse it got. Once she realized I wasn’t buying the act anymore, she didn’t tone it down – she turned it up. The charm dropped. The fake remorse disappeared. What was left was the monster: cold, unapologetic, and completely uninterested in pretending to care how much I was breaking.

That’s when life turned into literal hell. Every dial went to max: more gaslighting, more blame, more control, more cruelty. By that point I was too far in, too invested, too mentally shredded to just walk away. I had spent so much time and energy believing there was a real human with real emotions underneath the mask, I couldn’t accept that what I’d been loving was mostly an empty shell that mimicked feelings it had watched in other people.

I thought I could fix it. I thought I could love her hard enough to “beat” the narcissism or pull something real out of her. But all I did was sacrifice myself to an illusion. By the time I admitted the truth, I was already in a million pieces, alone with the job of trying to put myself back together without even remembering exactly where each piece went.

If you’re waiting for the version of them you met at the beginning to come back, ask yourself this: how many times has that mask shown up, just long enough to keep you hooked? And how much more of yourself are you willing to lose waiting for someone who was never really there?

Healing
Catharsis & Reclaiming Myself

Writing The Monster that lives on 2120 Samuel Drive became more than just making an album — it became a way to survive. Every song was a place to put the anger, fear, confusion, and grief that I couldn’t safely say out loud in that relationship.

Learning about narcissistic behavior gave me language for what I went through: gaslighting, trauma bonding, manipulation, control. Once I had the words, I could start to see the pattern. Once I saw the pattern, I could start to find a way out.

How This Album Helped Me Escape
  • It gave me a safe place to tell the truth, even when I still felt afraid.
  • It turned my experiences into something I could shape, instead of something that only shaped me.
  • It let me connect my mental health journey to my art, instead of just to my pain.
  • It reminded me that my voice still exists — and it’s mine.

Healing isn’t a straight line. I still live with ADHD, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia. I still have days where the past feels too close. But creating this album has been a step toward reclaiming myself from what happened on that street, in that house, with that person.

If you see yourself in any part of this story, I hope this album and this site remind you that you’re not alone, and that reaching out for help is an act of strength, not weakness.
